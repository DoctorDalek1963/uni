\question{1}

\begin{questionbody}
Let $(X, d)$ be a metric space and let $x \ne y$ be two elements of $X$. Show that $\exists \varepsilon_1, \varepsilon_2 > 0$ such that $\bb B(x, \varepsilon_1) \cap \bb B(y, \varepsilon_2) = \emptyset$.
\end{questionbody}

Choose $\varepsilon_1$ and $\varepsilon_2$ such that $\varepsilon_1 + \varepsilon_2 < d(x, y)$ and consider a point $z \in \bb B(x, \varepsilon_1) \cap \bb B(y, \varepsilon_2)$. Then $d(x, z) < \varepsilon_1$ and $d(y, z) < \varepsilon_2$. By the triangle inequality, $d(x, y) \le d(x, z) + d(y, z)$.

But $\varepsilon_1 + \varepsilon_2 < d(x, y)$ and $d(x, z) + d(y, z) < \varepsilon_1 + \varepsilon_2$. Therefore the triangle inequality would require $\varepsilon_1 + \varepsilon_2 < \varepsilon_1 + \varepsilon_2$. This is clearly impossible, and therefore no such $z$ can exist, so $\bb B(x, \varepsilon_1) \cap \bb B(y, \varepsilon_2) = \emptyset$.

\hfill $\square$

\newquestion{4}

\begin{questionbody}
Let $\cal T$ be a topology on $\R$. Suppose that for every pair of real numbers $a$ and $b$ with $a < b$, we have $[a, b] \in \cal T$. Show that $\cal T$ must be the discrete topology.
\end{questionbody}

To show that $\cal T$ is the discrete topology, we want to show that $\cal T = \cal P(\R)$.

Since $\cal T$ is a topology, finite intersections of elements of $\cal T$ are also elements of $\cal T$. Suppose $a < b < c$, then $[a, b] \cap [b, c] = \{b\} \in \cal T$. Using this technique, we know that for any $x \in \R$, $\{x\} \in \cal T$.

Since arbitrary (possibly infinite) unions of elements of $\cal T$ are also elements of $\cal T$, \[
\bigcup_{a < x < b} \{x\} = (a, b) \in \cal T.
\] More generally, any $U \subset \cal P(\R)$ can be constructed as a union of singletons. Since $\cal T$ contains all singletons, it contains all such $U$ and therefore $\cal T = \cal P(\R)$ as required.

\hfill $\square$
