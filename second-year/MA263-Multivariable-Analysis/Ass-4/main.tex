\question{1}

\begin{questionbody}
Let $A \subset {[0, 1]}^2$ and let $f : {[0, 1]}^2 \to \R$ be such that $f(x) = 1$ for $x \in A$ and zero otherwise.

Suppose that $f$ is not integrable. Show that there exists $\varepsilon > 0$ so that whenever $P$ is a partition of ${[0, 1]}^2$, then \[
\sum_{\substack{S \in RP \\ S \cap \partial A \ne \emptyset}} v(S) \ge \varepsilon,
\] where $\partial A$ is the topological boundary of $A$.
\end{questionbody}

Since $f$ is not integrable, the Riemann integrability condition does not hold. The condition states: `for all $\varepsilon > 0$, there exists a partition $P$ such that $U(f, P) - L(f, P) < \varepsilon$'. We negate this statement and observe that there exists $\varepsilon > 0$ such that for all partitions $P$, $U(f, P) - L(f, P) \ge \varepsilon$. Now we just have to show that \[
U(f, P) - L(f, P) \ge \varepsilon \implies \sum_{\substack{S \in RP \\ S \cap \partial A \ne \emptyset}} v(S) \ge \varepsilon.
\]

The LHS just says \[
\sum_{S \in RP} v(S) \sup_{x \in S} f(x) - \sum_{S \in RP} v(S) \inf_{x \in S} f(x) \ge \varepsilon,
\] so \[
\sum_{S \in RP} v(S) \l( \sup_{x \in S} f(x) - \inf_{x \in S} f(x) \r) \ge \varepsilon.
\]

Since $f(x) = 1$ when $x \in A$ and $f(x) = 0$ otherwise, $\sup_{x \in S} f(x) = 1$ if and only if $S \cap A \ne \emptyset$, otherwise 0, and $\inf_{x \in S} f(x) = 0$ if and only if $S \cap A^\complement \ne \emptyset$, otherwise 1. Therefore, \[
\sup_{x \in S} f(x) - \inf_{x \in S} f(x) = \begin{cases}
1 & \text{if } S \cap \partial A \ne \emptyset, \\
0 & \text{otherwise}.
\end{cases}
\] Therefore, \[
\sum_{S \in RP} v(S) \l( \sup_{x \in S} f(x) - \inf_{x \in S} f(x) \r) = \sum_{\substack{S \in RP \\ S \cap \partial A \ne \emptyset}} v(S) \ge \varepsilon.
\]
\hfill $\square$

\newquestion{2}

\begin{questionbody}
Consider the function $f : {[0, 1]}^2 \to \R$ defined by \[
f(x, y) = \begin{cases}
xy & \text{if } x \ge y, \\
0 & \text{otherwise}.
\end{cases}
\] Show that $f$ is integrable.
\end{questionbody}

First, let $K = \l\{(x, y) \in {[0, 1]}^2 : x \ge y\r\}$ and define $g : K \to \R$ to be $g(x, y) = xy$. This is a continuous function on a closed and bounded domain, so $g$ is uniformly continuous. By year 1 analysis, a uniformly continuous function is integrable, so $g$ is integrable on $K$.

Since $f$ is the extension of $g$ to ${[0, 1]}^2$ where $f(x) = 0$ for $x \notin K$, \[
\int_{{[0, 1]}^2} f = \int_K g,
\] so $f$ is integrable.

\hfill $\square$
