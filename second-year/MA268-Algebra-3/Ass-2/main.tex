\question{1}

\begin{questionbody}
Let $n \ge 3$. Recall that $D_{2n} = \grpprs{r, s}{r^n = s^2 = \mathrm{id}, srs = r^{-1}}$. We follow the convention of writing elements of $D_{2n}$ as $r^k$ or $sr^k$ where $k$ only matters modulo~$n$.
\begin{enumerate}[(i)]
\item Show that $r^k \cdot s = sr^{-k}$.

\item Complete the following multiplication rules for $D_{2n}$:
\[
\setlength{\extrarowheight}{3pt}% local setting
\begin{array}{c||c|c} % chktex 44
    & r^k & s r^k \\
\hline \hline % chktex 44
r^\ell && \\
\hline % chktex 44
s r^\ell &&
\end{array}
\]
\end{enumerate}
\end{questionbody}

\subsection{~} % 1.i

We know that $srs = r^{-1}$, so we can derive that $rs = s^{-1} r^{-1} = sr^{-1}$, since $s$ is self-inverse. Then we have
% \begin{align*}
% r^k s &= \underbrace{r \dotsm r}_{k \text{ times}} s \\
% &= \underbrace{r \dotsm r}_{k-1 \text{ times}} s r^{-1} \\
% &= \underbrace{r \dotsm r}_{k-2 \text{ times}} s r^{-1} r^{-1} \\
% % &\quad \vdots \\
% &= s \underbrace{r^{-1} \dotsm r^{-1}}_{k \text{ times}} \\
% &= sr^{-k}
% \end{align*}
\begin{align*}
r^k s &= r^{k-1} r s \\
&= r^{k-1} s r^{-1} \\
&= r^{k-2} r s r^{-1} \\
&= r^{k-2} s r^{-2} \\
&\,\ \vdots \\
&= s r^{-k}
\end{align*}

\subsection{~} % 1.ii

\[
\setlength{\extrarowheight}{3pt}% local setting
\begin{array}{c||c|c} % chktex 44
    & r^k & s r^k \\
\hline \hline % chktex 44
r^\ell & r^{\ell + k} & sr^{k - \ell} \\
\hline % chktex 44
s r^\ell & sr^{\ell + k} & r^{k - \ell}
\end{array}
\]

\newquestion{2}

\begin{questionbody}
Determine all homomorphisms $D_{2n} \to \mathbb{C}^*$.
\end{questionbody}

To preserve the cyclic nature of rotations, we need to have $\phi(r) = \e^{2 i \pi k / n}$ for some $k \in \{1, \dotsc, n\}$.

However, we cannot preserve the relation that $srs = r^{-1}$, since it requires some sort of reflection. Since every element of $\C^*$ geometrically represents a rotation, we cannot preserve reflections. Thus, no homomorphisms exists from $D_{2n}$ to $\C^*$.

\newquestion{3}

\begin{questionbody}
Recall the rotation matrix $R_\theta = \begin{pmatrix} \cos \theta & -\sin \theta \\ \sin \theta & \cos \theta \end{pmatrix}$. This represents anti-clockwise rotation through angle $\theta$. It is obvious geometrically, and easy to check using trig identities that $R_\theta R_\phi = R_{\theta + \phi}$. Let $S = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix}$.
\begin{enumerate}[(i)]
\item What does multiplying a vector by $S$ do geometrically?

\item Show that there is a unique homomorphism \[ \rho : D_{2n} \to \mathrm{GL}_2(\R), \qquad \rho(r) = R_{2\pi/n}, \qquad \rho(s) = S. \]
\end{enumerate}
\end{questionbody}

\subsection{~} % 3.i

Multiplying a vector by $S$ reflects it in the $x$-axis.

\subsection{~} % 3.ii

Clearly $\rho$ is a homomorphism since it preserves the relations that define $D_{2n}$: \[ r^n = s^2 = \mathrm{id}, srs = r^{-1} \]
\begin{align*}
\rho(r^n) &= \rho(\mathrm{id}) \\
&= I \\
&= {(R_{2\pi/n})}^n \\
&= {\rho(r)}^n \\[1ex]
%
\rho(s^2) &= \rho(\mathrm{id}) \\
&= I \\
&= S^2 \\
&= {\rho(s)}^2 \\[1ex]
%
\rho(srs) &= \rho(r^{-1}) \\
&= {(R_{2\pi/n})}^{-1} \\
&= R_{-2\pi/n} \\
&= S R_{2\pi/n} S \\
&= \rho(s) \rho(r) \rho(s)
\end{align*}

By the Fundamental Theorem of Group Presentations, this homomorphism is unique.

\setlength{\extrarowheight}{3pt}% local setting
\begin{array}{c||*{3}{c|}c} % chktex 44
    & y^k & y^k x & y^k x^2 & y^k x^3 \\
\hline \hline % chktex 44
y^\ell &&&& \\

\hline % chktex 44

\hline % chktex 44

\begin{align*}

\newquestion{5}

\begin{questionbody}
\begin{enumerate}[(i)]
\item Let $n \ge 3$. Determine the elements of order 2 in $D_{2n}$.

\item Determine the elements of order 2 in $Q_8$.

\item Show that $Q_8 \not\cong D_{2n}$ for all $n \ge 3$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{questionbody}

\subsection{~} % 3.i

Clearly all reflections are order 2, and there are $n$ reflections. If $n$ is even, then $r^{n/2}$ is also of order 2. Therefore there are $n$ or $n+1$ elements of order 2 in $D_{2n}$.

\subsection{~} % 3.ii

We know that \[
Q_8 = \grpprs{a, b}{a^4 = \mathrm{id}, a^2 = b^2, b a b^{-1} = a^{-1}}
\] and we know from Lemma~V.5.2 in the lectures that every element of $Q_8$ can be written uniquely as $a^i b^j$ for some $0 \le i \le 3$ and $0 \le j \le 1$.

Since $b a b^{-1} = a^{-1}$ and $a^4 = \mathrm{id}$, we get $ba = a^3 b$.

% To have an element of order 2, we would need ${(a^i b^j)}^2 = 1$. Write $j = 2r + s$ where $s = 1$ or 0. Then we have
% \begin{align*}
% a^i b^j &= a^i b^j \\
% &= a^i b^{2r + s} a^i b^{2r + s} \\
% &= a^i b^{2r + s} \\
% &= a^i {(b^2)}^r b^s \\
% &= a^i a^r b^s \\
% &= a^{i + r} b^s
% \end{align*}
% And so
% \begin{align*}
% % {(a^i b^j)}^2 &= a^i b^j a^i b^j \\
% % &= a^i b^{2r + s} a^i b^{2r + s} \\
% % &= a^i {(b^2)}^r b^s a^i {(b^2)}^r b^s \\
% % &= a^i a^r b^s a^i a^r b^s \\
% % &= a^{i + r} b^s a^{i + r} b^s \\
% {(a^i b^j)}^2 &= {(a^{i + r} b^s)}^2 \\
% &= a^{i + r} b^s a^{i + r} b^s
% \end{align*}
% In the case of $s=0$, we just have ${(a^{i+r})}^2$. In the case of $s=1$, we have
% \begin{align*}
% a^{i + r} b a^{i + r} b &= a^{i + r} a^3 b a^{i + r - 1} b \\
% &= a^{i + r} a^6 b a^{i + r - 2} b \\
% &\,\ \vdots \\
% &= a^{i + r} a^{3(i + r)} b^2 \\
% &= {(a^{i + r})}^4 b^2 \\
% &= \mathrm{id}
% \end{align*}

Since there are only 8 elements of $Q_8$, we can just square all of them and see which ones have order 2. We will skip $a^0 b^0$ however, since we know this has order 1.
\begin{align*}
{(a^1 b^0)}^2 &= a^2 \\
{(a^2 b^0)}^2 &= a^4 = \mathrm{id} \\
{(a^3 b^0)}^2 &= a^6 = a^2 \\
{(a^0 b^1)}^2 &= b^2 = a^2 \\
{(a^1 b^1)}^2 &= abab \\
&= a a^3 b b \\
&= a^2 \\
{(a^2 b^1)}^2 &= a^2 b a^2 b \\
&= a^2 (b a) a b \\
&= a^2 (a^3 b) a b \\
&= a (b a) b \\
&= a (a^3 b) b \\
&= a^2 \displaybreak \\
{(a^3 b^1)}^2 &= a^3 b a^3 b \\
&= a^3 (b a) a^2 b \\
&= a^3 (a^3 b) a^2 b \\
&= a^2 (b a) a b \\
&= a^2 (a^3 b) a b \\
&= a (b a) b \\
&= a (a^3 b) b \\
&= a^2
\end{align*}
Thus the only element of order 2 is $a^2$.

\subsection{~} % 3.iii

Since $n \ge 3$, the number of elements in $D_{2n}$ of order two must be at least 3. But $Q_8$ only has one element of order 2, so $Q_8$ cannot be isomorphic to $D_{2n}$.
